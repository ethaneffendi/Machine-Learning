Machine Learning

Imagine being tasked with writing a program that can recognize the face of some woman named Jane. One approach is to spend hours looking at pictures of her her to extract distinguishing facial characteristics. An algorithm can then be written based on those characteristics to receive a picture of a face, and after analyzing it using hard coded instructions, determine whether it is of Jane or not.

Imagine if every smartphone with face unlocking capabilities were designed this way. Customers would take hundreds of pictures of themselves and send them to the phone manufacturer. After months of writing a program that can recognize that person's face, an engineer would ship the finished product out for a hefty sum. This approach simply is not sustainable. It is neither consistent nor efficient!

Luckily, a better approach exists. It's called machine learning. The premise of machine learning is providing a computer with two things: an algorithm and plenty of data. Using the algorithm, the computer is to learn patterns in the provided dataset. Knowing patterns, such as the those that distinguish whether a picture is of Jane or some stranger, computers have a reusable way of tackling computing problems that previously required hard coded solutions.

When I was initially exposed to the idea of machine learning in 2022, I stumbled upon MIT's 6.036 Introduction to Machine Learning lectures online. The university uploads plenty of its own learning materials to its website, providing anyone with internet access with quality learning resources. I have repeatedly watched the lectures in my free time, often restarting after months-long pauses (life happens). However, Professor Kaelbling's lectures have each time afforded me hours of incredibly interesting questions and thinking. This site stores my notes on her lectures in addition to projects I performed to put theories in the lectures to a test.
