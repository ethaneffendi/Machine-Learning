Machine Learning

Imagine being tasked with writing a program that can recognize the face of some woman named Jane. One approach is to spend hours looking at pictures of her to extract distinguishing facial characteristics. An algorithm can then be written based on those characteristics to receive a picture of a face, and after analyzing it using hard coded instructions, determine whether it is of Jane or not.

Imagine if every smartphone with face unlocking capabilities were designed this way. Customers would have to take hundreds of pictures of themselves and send them to a phone manufacturer. Only after months of writing custom, hard coded programs that can recognize a particular person's face could a company ship one phone. This approach is simply unsustainable. It is inconsistent, expensive, and time-consuming.

Luckily, a better approach exists. It's called machine learning. The premise of machine learning is providing a computer with two things: an algorithm and plenty of data. Using the algorithm, the computer is to learn patterns in the provided data. Knowing patterns, such as the those that distinguish whether a picture is of Jane or some stranger, computers have a reusable way of tackling computing problems that previously required hard coded solutions.

Supervised Learning

Based on the problem one is attempting to solve, various types of machine learning exist. However, supervised learning is a great place to start. It's relatively intuitive to grasp, and it provides many foundations on which other kinds of machine learning are built.
